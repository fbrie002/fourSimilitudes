1. Convenientia


"Convenientia denotes the adjacency of places. Those things are ‘convenient’ which come sufficiently close to one another to be in juxtaposition; their edges touch, their fringes intermingle, the extremity of the one also denotes the beginning of the other. So that in this hinge between two things a resemblance appears.
A resemblance that becomes double as soon as one attempts to unravel it: (...) a similitude of properties; for in this natural container, the world, adjacency is not an exterior relation between things, but the sign of a relationship, obscure though it may be.

In the vast syntax of the world, the different beings adjust themselves to one another; the plant communicates with the animal, the earth with the sea, man with everything around him".

- Foucault, Michel. The Order of Things: An Archaeology of the Human Sciences. New York, NY: Pantheon Books, 1970.
